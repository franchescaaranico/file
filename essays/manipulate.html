disclaimer
These are just products of overanalyzing people's behavior to an arguably unhealthy degree. I could be wrong about a few things, and some of these concepts may already have proper psychological terms that I simply do not know. This is just my attempt at making sense of patterns I've noticed in manipulative people, in questionable relationships, and, at times, in myself. One thing I am sure, You cannot build something healthy out of playing stupid mind games. There are a few reasons you might be reading this. One, you are just bored and happened to stumble upon this by accident. Two, you are a piece of shit who wants to use the information here to manipulate someone. Three, you want to be aware of how manipulation works so you can protect yourself from it. Whatever category you fall into, welcome.

intro
I think manipulation, in itself, is not automatically evil. Even babies do it all the time to get what they want, and most people manipulate situations to some degree without even realizing it. As for me personally, I often do it to survive social dynamics. I tweak my behavior and personality depending on who I am with at the moment, to be more palatable. That is why I think manipulation is not inherently evil, it depends on your intentions. It starts becoming evil when it is used to exploit, control, or deceive someone for personal gain. I have also been a victim of these kinds of manipulation. Normal advice would probably be, Stay away from manipulative people. But, I've never been very good at following normal advice. Oftentimes, I stayed because I was curious. I wanted to see how far they would go to ruin me, to see what kind of twisted connection we'd develop that would be all-consuming enough to send me to therapy. I wanted to understand how their minds worked, and without realizing it, I found myself becoming more like them as self-protection. Fortunately, self-awareness was my antidote against descent.

common victims of manipulation
One of the reasons why manipulation works because it exploits human needs. Manipulators identify whichever need is strongest in you and position themselves as the solution. If you're lonely, they become your source of connection. If you're insecure, they become your source of validation. Once they become the sole supplier of something you emotionally depend on, they gain influence over you. Many manipulators act as blank canvases. They mirror your desires, values, and dreams back to you. You think you've found your soulmate when, in reality, you're looking at a reflection of your own fantasies. The manipulator simply knows how to become whatever you want them to be.

One common trait among victims is low self-esteem. If, deep down, you don't think highly of yourself, flattery and love bombing become very effective. The manipulator feeds you a version of yourself that feels desirable and special. So it becomes really hard for you to discern that they are manipulators, because that would also mean losing the idealized version of yourself that they helped create. Another mistake people make is revealing their entire dating blueprint too early. Never tell the person you are dating every detail of your ideal partner. A manipulative person may simply take notes and become exactly what you described. As a result, it can feel as though you've found your perfect match, when in reality they may simply be altering themselves to create the illusion of compatibility.

Another group of ideal victims is people who don’t know themselves very well. If you have no strong sense of identity, you become dependent on external validation. This is where push-and-pull tactics become effective. The manipulator alternates between affection and withdrawal until your self-worth becomes tied to their approval. You start chasing their validation like an addict chasing their next high. Emotionally vulnerable people are easy targets too. When you're grieving, lonely, heartbroken, depressed, or desperate for connection, your guard naturally comes down. This is why making fake future promises works so well, since victims are often not just drawn to the manipulator themselves. They also become obssessed by the future the manipulator paints for them, the vision of a different life, or a more fulfilled version of who they could be.

List of behaviours I have noticed that manipulative people commonly exhibit. I want to emphasize that observing one of these behaviors in isolation does not automatically make someone manipulative. Context always matters. However, if someone consistently displays several of these patterns over time and their actions appear calculated, it can reasonably be considered a red flag.

emotional association
This happens when someone repeatedly associates their presence with your strongest emotional moments. For example, they only show up when you're very sad. They help you and make you feel better. Over time, your brain begins linking emotional relief with their presence. I used to do this unintentionally. I would only appear when someone was struggling and I would disappear and ignore them when things were going well because I assumed they no longer needed me. Looking back, I realize it created unhealthy, obsessive attachments that I never intended, where people misidentified me as the source of their happiness and mistook that feeling for being in love with me. I’ve learned that when people are depressed, it is fine to give them some encouragement, but never position yourself as the savior from their misery. Not only are you hijacking their healing process, but you are also preventing them from growing out of that situation by placing yourself at the center as the solution. Another example is when someone shows up only during your strongest positive emotional states. This can trick you into believing that their presence caused the happiness you are feeling, even though they are not necessarily the source of those emotions.

asking little favors
This can be seen as one way people use small favors as a subtle form of manipulation. It’s based on a real psychological phenomenon, when someone does small favors for another person, they may start to feel a slight increase in liking toward them due to consistency and cognitive dissonance. I found that the correct term for this is the “Ben Franklin effect.” However, in my opinion, people who believe this tactic will reliably make others like them are delulu. First of all, it doesn’t override genuine compatibility or an existing lack of interest. So if someone doesn’t like you from the beginning, no amount of small favors you ask of them will make them like you. It’s only effective when there is already baseline attraction and when the interaction feels natural. Otherwise, it can backfire if the favors feel calculated or excessive.

"you vs. ideal type” comparison
This is a very specific action, an indirect rejection disguised as flirting. When asked a question like “What kind of person do you like?”, a manipulative individual may deliberately describe traits that contrast with the person asking. For example, if the person is skinny and white, the manipulator might respond, “I like someone who is a bit chubby and tan.” This can trigger insecurity or a desire for approval, leading the person to try to change or prove themselves, because their self-esteem has been hurt, even if they are not yet sure if they really like the manipulator. This type of manipulation does not work if someone has a secure sense of self, in that case, hearing that they are not the other person’s type would simply make them walk away. The tactic only works by exploiting an insecure person’s sensitivity to perceived rejection.

unavailability effect
It is fascinating to me that many people find unavailable individuals attractive, whether they live on a different continent, are already married, or are separated by class barriers. The harder someone is to obtain, the more desirable they appear, and this sense of restriction often creates obsession. Manipulators may deliberately manufacture a sense of unavailability in order to make others become fixated on them. Examples, "The age gap between us is too wide for a relationship," "You are too far away," "People think we look like siblings," or other external obstacles to a possible relationship. In my opinion, particularly from a woman’s perspective (who is often exposed to romantic media while growing up) we are taught to believe that true love is meant to be very difficult to attain. Because of this, distance and impossibility seem very alluring, as we may interpret them as signs that someone is “the one.” We see this theme repeatedly in literature, like Romeo and Juliet and Pride and Prejudice. The most celebrated romances are often those that face major obstacles. In modern times, I notice that people convince themselves their love is stronger simply because a real relationship is not possible. It is the very impossibility that makes the appeal so strong. Nevertheless, this tactic is unlikely to work on two kinds of people. (1) those who have no baseline attraction toward you, as they would likely be glad you left them alone, and (2) those who are emotionally secure. In the latter case, hearing that they are not meant to be together would only lead them to move on to someone else.

in defense of "you vs. ideal type” comparison and unavailability effect
I think it's important to emphasize that while these two behaviors are commonly associated with manipulators, people-pleasers can sometimes do them unintentionally. What I've noticed is that the "you vs. ideal type" comparison can look very similar to indirect rejection, and unavailability effect can resemble a harmless rejection. These are two things I used to do quite often when rejecting people. Because I was afraid of hurting their feelings, when someone pursuing me asked what type of person I wanted, I would deliberately describe the opposite of them. I thought this would signal them to go away, or I would come up with reasons for rejection that were outside of their control so they wouldn't feel like there was something wrong with them. However, I noticed that instead of backing away, many of them became even more eager to pursue me. That led me to the conclusion that indirect and seemingly harmless rejection only creates confusion and false hope. Therefore, it is better to be honest and give a direct rejection, even if you know it will hurt them. It seems to me that there is no such thing as a painless rejection.

expectation setting
Giving explanations before anyone asks, in order to shape how future behavior is interpreted. This occurs when someone provides preemptive justifications that frame their actions before any uncertainty or questioning arises. One example was when I dated this guy, and he would often give information I never asked for, in a way that set expectations within our connection. He would say, “I usually don’t reply after 5 PM until the next morning because I’m at the gym, and by the time I get home I’m exhausted and forget about my phone.” Now, whenever he disappeared for hours or days, I would automatically interpret his behavior through the narrative he had already provided. Instead of trusting my instincts, I defaulted to a pre-existing explanation that had been implanted in advance. And spoiler alert, he was in a parallel relationship, and his unavailability during those times was because he was with her. To be fair, this behavior is not always deceptive. For some people, myself included, this can stem from an avoidant attachment style. For example, we may over-explain when we are available or unavailable in order to reassure the other person that they are not being avoided and to prevent future miscommunication.

love bombing
It is an act of overwhelming someone with affection and attention. The goal is to create emotional attachment quickly, wherein the person feels seen and special. The problem arises when the affection disappears once the manipulator gets what they want. For example, a person may do this for sexual gain. They may spend long hours with you during the first meetings, showering you with compliments and gifts. Once you let your guard down and have sex, they may disappear into oblivion, leaving you feeling emotionally discarded. Anyway, this tactic has never worked on me, those who try it usually end up disappointed.

making future promises
This involves painting an exciting picture of a future that may never actually happen. The victim becomes attached not only to the person, but also to the life they imagine having with them. In many cases, people are grieving the fantasy more than the relationship itself. A manipulative person might talk about marriage, describe what your future children would look like, discuss places you'll travel together, or casually include you in long-term plans. These conversations create a sense of certainty and emotional investment long before the future has been earned. This tactic is especially effective when someone is unhappy with their current life. If you feel lonely, stuck, or dissatisfied with your present circumstances, those promises can begin to feel like your only source of hope. Letting go of the manipulator then becomes difficult because it would also mean losing the future they convinced you was waiting for you.

jealousy tactics
This involves creating a competitive or comparative dynamic. I often see petty people intentionally do this to make their partner jealous and get a reaction from them. Mentioning other romantic interests, exes, or people who desire them creates insecurity and makes the other person work harder for approval. Examples like “Someone asked me out just yesterday,” “My ex used to plan really thoughtful dates,” and “It’s funny how many people are sliding into my DMs right now.” Those kinds of statements, even without naming anyone, suggest that there are alternatives.

reflective listening
This technique involves repeating what someone says in your own words. It makes people feel deeply understood and validated. The behavior itself is not inherently manipulative, empathetic friends and even therapists often use it to encourage people to open up and explore their feelings. However, it is also a tactic that manipulators commonly use to build trust quickly.

suggestive framing
If reflective listening does not work, another way of getting people to open up is through implicit suggestion, framing the situation so it would seem like it is safer or more beneficial for them to share. This can create the impression that opening up is entirely their choice, while subtly steering them toward that outcome. For example: “You don’t have to tell me if it’s confidential, but if you do, I’m always here to listen.” The statement gives the illusion of freedom while gently encouraging disclosure, making the person feel in control even though the response has already been guided by how the question was framed from the start.

giving the illusion of choice
Again, this isn’t inherently a manipulative action in itself. Empathetic friends may use similar approaches to help you open up. However, if sufficient rapport hasn’t been established and someone uses it anyway, it can be a red flag. Another sentence structure commonly used to encourage disclosure is, “Do you want to open up now or later?” On the surface, it appears to give the person a choice, but both options ultimately lead to the same outcome, its only a matter of now or later. The person is still being prompted to open up. The sense of choice can make the interaction feel voluntary, even though the underlying goal remains unchanged regardless of the response.

flattery
After the manipulator succeeded in making you open up to them, either through suggestive framing, reflective listening, or by giving the illusion of choice, and now you finally revealed all your insecurities and deepest fears and childhood wounds, they will now use this next technique, commonly known as flattery. Manipulators have already studied your insecurities and will now tell you exactly what you want to hear. They make you feel that nobody understands you except them. At first, they soothe your wounds. Later, when they're angry or no longer getting what they want, they may weaponize those same vulnerabilities.

push and pull
It’s a behavior that creates uncertainty. The unpredictability keeps the victim emotionally invested because they’re constantly trying to figure out how to get the person’s attention and validation again. This is one of the more addictive manipulation tactics. The manipulator alternates between affection and distance. One day they’re warm and loving, the next they’re cold and unavailable. The main reason this works is, say for example, if you have someone who frequently comments on your posts or someone you talk to all the time, and then they suddenly go silent. Your mind starts searching for their presence, replaying interactions, and overthinking what you might have done wrong, often pushing you to do things you normally wouldn't just to get their attention. The unpredictability of their disappearance after establishing an emotional bond is what makes you miss them even more than before. Some people do it intentionally because they know that inconsistency creates anxiety and attachment. Meanwhile, other people...others are just busy and have a life.
