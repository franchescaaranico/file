flirting as emotional generosity

May 15, 2026

Recently, I had a realization about how different it is when someone says they like you versus when you’re simply a likable person. The difference, I think, has a lot to do with flirting. I’ve started seeing flirting as a kind of play adults perform with each other. At its best, it’s light and communal. I think the world would feel a lot warmer if more people flirted, to remind others that they are interesting, attractive, and worth noticing. And that is not to say that everyone should be trying to get with everyone.

I learned that when someone teases you playfully, calls you pet names, or gives specific compliments about your appearance, personality, talents, and so on, it doesn’t automatically mean they’re romantically interested, which is the part people often confuse. It is simply an acknowledgment, a way of saying, I see you, and you’re delightful to be around. A person can enjoy your company deeply without wanting to build a life with you. They can admire you without intending possession.

Flirting is a way of saying you are a likable person, and I want you to feel that. Maybe I actually like you and even want to ravish you naked, but I don’t have to act on it. And if I don’t, it doesn’t mean there’s something wrong with you. It’s just about things outside of us.

In that sense, flirting can be a kind of emotional generosity. However, there’s another kind of flirting too, the type that feels intoxicating at first and hollow afterward. If the first can be called good flirting, this one might be bad flirting.

Bad flirting is often less about you and more about the flirter’s own hunger. It’s when someone seems intensely into you, but you realize there are five other people receiving the exact same treatment. You thought you were special, but that’s just their default mode. They do this because they’re trying to fill something within themselves, which I would assume is an attachment wound.

People who feel fundamentally unlovable sometimes overextend themselves emotionally. They become experts at manufacturing closeness quickly through charm, affection, seduction, and over-disclosure. Flirting becomes a shortcut to create the illusion of intimacy because real intimacy is slow and something they don’t actually want to commit to.

And often, what they’re really chasing is reciprocity. They give out the love they never got to receive, hoping someone will hand it back to them, fast and full, even if it’s just for one night. And they are prepared for the possibility that you won’t be seeing each other the next morning, and maybe never again.
